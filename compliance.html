Why This Matters

Regulation moved the burden of proof
onto the buyer.

A decade ago, a European company could buy from a supplier and take their word for it. That era has ended.

Under the EU Batteries Regulation, the EU Deforestation Regulation and the incoming Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, the company placing goods on the European market carries the legal obligation to prove those goods are traceable, legally produced and free of human rights abuse. Fail, and you lose market access.

That proof cannot be assembled from a desk in Europe. It has to be gathered at source, by people who can visit the sites, speak the languages and be trusted by the communities. That is exactly where we work.

Where the Pressure Is Highest

Cobalt is where this regulation bites first.

Focus Sector

Around 70% of the world's cobalt originates in the Democratic Republic of Congo. It sits in every electric vehicle, phone and laptop battery sold in Europe.

Under the EU Batteries Regulation, any company placing batteries on the European market must prove that cobalt is responsibly sourced, traceable, and free of human rights abuse. The obligation falls on the importer. But the evidence sits with the producer, thousands of miles away, in a language they do not speak.

That gap is the whole problem. Closing it is the whole service.

We support both sides of it: Congolese exporters who must produce the evidence, and European buyers who must hold it. ICONGRP has an established operation in Kinshasa and a UK compliance team that knows what the regulators will accept.

Beyond Cobalt

The same model applies wherever the burden of proof outruns the available evidence.

Cobalt under the Batteries Regulation is where the pressure is greatest today. The EU Deforestation Regulation places identical obligations on timber, cocoa, coffee, rubber, soy, palm oil and cattle. The CSDDD extends the principle across entire corporate supply chains. As our clients' obligations widen, so does our practice.
